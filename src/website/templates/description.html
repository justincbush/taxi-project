The Basic Problem

There are two obvious factors that determine how quickly a taxi can take you between two points in New York City. First, you have to find an available cab. Then you have to make your way through traffic.

From publicly available data, we have detailed information about every taxi trip taken in New York City during 2013. In particular we have the latitude and longitude coordinates as well as the precise time for the beginning and end of each of the more than 170 million trips. Well, "precise" but of course their are outliers, and obviously incorrect data mixed in. Once we've accounted for that, however, we can use this data to get information about how long cab trips between different parts of the city actually take, and how it depends on the time of day.

Pushing the data a little harder, we can make inferences about the number of available cabs in a given part of the city at different times during the day. I say "pushing" because there is no data about when a cab in on or off duty, or where the cabs are driving when they don't have passengers. But if we can handle those problems sensibly, then we can imagine the number of available cabs should correspond to how easy it is to hail a taxi, and we can represent that information on a map as I've done in the images on the right.

Estimating Trip Times

Let's first take the more straightforward problem of estimating trip times between different locations in the city at a particular time and day of the week. The basic approach is to divide the city up into a grid based on latitude and longitude coordinates. There is a careful balance to strike here, between making the grid small enough to get as detailed information as possible, while large enough to ensure there are a meaningful number of trips between grid elements. Using the whole city as one big grid element is boring and uninformative, while using a grid that's just a few feet across means you won't have any similar trips to use in comparison.

For the taxi data, the grid that gave the best tradeoff was 1/50 of a degree on each side. This is still fairly large---larger than a small Manhattan neighborhood, for instance. So directly reporting the distribution of trip times between two grid elements still might not be very informative. But we can actually do better than that. While we might expect a trip starting several blocks away and going to the same destination to take a different amount of time, we might be more willing to expect it to go at roughly the same speed. From this observation, we can see that what we really want to keep track of are the distributions of speeds (and not times) between different parts of the city. Given that we know the distance of our trip, once we know the distribution of speeds we can infer the distribution of times.

Estimating Cab Availability

Estimating how difficult it is to hail a cab is a little trickier. We don't have any data for how long people had to wait to get a ride (or how many people gave up looking). But based on the number of cabs in an area, how much time those cabs go between fares, and how quickly the traffic is moving, we can gain a sense of the average difficulty facing someone looking for a ride.
